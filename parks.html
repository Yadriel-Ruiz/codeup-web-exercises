Parks List

Yellowstone

  • World's First National Park: Established in 1872, Yellowstone is not only the first national park in the United States but also the first national park in the world. This historic designation paved the way for the establishment of protected areas globally.

  • Geothermal Marvels: Yellowstone is home to over half of the world's geothermal features, including the famous Old Faithful geyser. The park sits atop a volcanic hotspot, creating a unique landscape of geysers, hot springs, and mud pots.

  • Wildlife Paradise: Yellowstone is a wildlife enthusiast's dream. It's one of the last places in the U.S. where you can see free-ranging herds of bison and elk. The park is also home to grizzly bears, wolves, and a variety of other wildlife species.

  • Super Volcano: Beneath Yellowstone lies a supervolcano, one of the largest active volcanic systems in the world. While the chances of a catastrophic eruption are extremely low, the geothermal activity is a testament to the park's geological significance.

Haleakalā

  • Spectacular Sunrise: Haleakalā is renowned for its breathtaking sunrise views. Visitors often wake up early to witness the sunrise from the summit, where the colors of the sky and the volcanic landscape create a surreal and magical experience.

  • Endangered Species: The park is home to several endangered species, including the nēnē (Hawaiian goose), which has made a remarkable recovery thanks to conservation efforts. The unique environment of Haleakalā provides a sanctuary for these rare species.

  • Martian Landscape: The summit area of Haleakalā has an otherworldly appearance, often likened to the surface of Mars. NASA astronauts even trained here in the 1960s to prepare for the lunar landings due to the crater's resemblance to the moon's surface.

  • House of the Sun: The name Haleakalā translates to "House of the Sun" in Hawaiian. According to Hawaiian mythology, the demigod Maui captured the sun and forced it to slow its journey across the sky, making the days longer.

Crater Lake

  • Emerging about 160 feet above the surface of Crater Lake, this 300 foot island resembles a sailing ship. The Phantom Ship is composed of weathered andesite pinnacles about 400,000 years old. The formation is probably the remains of a filled fissure which was exposed with the caldera collapse of Mount Mazama nearly 8,000 years ago. Depending on the weather conditions, the Phantom Ship seems to appear and disappear from sight, giving the formation its otherworldly nickname.

  • Crater Lake has inspired people of all ages for decades. In 1870, William Gladstone Steel — a young child from Kansas — read about Crater Lake in the newspaper. Fascinated by this amazing natural wonder, he vowed to visit the lake one day. Steel first visited Crater Lake in 1885, traveling by railroad and stagecoach before walking another 20 miles to finally view the incredible wonder. After fulfilling his dream, he made it his mission to make Crater Lake a national park, protecting and preserving its natural resources for generations to come. As a journalist, he naturally wrote about the lake's natural wonders, and soon began leading nature hikes so that important figures would be able to see why the wonderful lake should be protected. Steel's lobbying led to the designation of Crater Lake National Park as the sixth US National Park in 1902, and he became known as the "father of Crater Lake" and a "one man chamber of commerce".

  • You may even know the Mazama Newt more commonly as the Crater Lake Newt - as this species can only be found around the national park.

  • At 1,943 feet, Crater Lake is the deepest lake in the United States and one of the deepest in the world. The depths were first explored in 1886 by a group from the U.S. Geological Survey. Their sounding device consisted of a lead pipe attached to piano wire. After lowering it into the water at 168 locations around the lake, they determined the deepest part was 1,996 feet. Amazingly, this was only 53 feet shy of the official depth measurement recorded with multibeam side scan sonar in 2000. Impressive!

How many people visit the national parks?
Total recreation visitors to the national parks in 2018: 318,211,833.
How old is the National Park System?
The National Park Service was created by an act signed by President Woodrow Wilson on August 25, 1916.
How many employees are in the National Park Service?
Permanent, temporary, and seasonal employees: More than 20,000
What is the origin of the National Park Service arrowhead?
The arrowhead was authorized as the official National Park Service emblem by the Secretary of the Interior on July 20, 1951.
How many areas are in the National Park System?
The system includes 419 areas covering more than 85 million acres in every state, the District of Columbia, American Samoa, Guam, Puerto Rico, and the Virgin Islands.
What is the largest national park site?
Wrangell-St. Elias National Park and Preserve, AK, at 13.2 million acres.
What is the National Park Service budget?
Fiscal Year (FY) 2014 Enacted: $2.98 billion.
Can I bring my pet to a national park?
Some national parks welcome pets—in developed areas, on many trails and campgrounds, and in some lodging facilities.
How do I apply for a job with the National Park Service?
National Park Service jobs, including both permanent and seasonal positions, are listed on USAJOBs.
What do I need to know about driving off-road in national parks?
Before you head out, check with the national parks that you intend to visit. In many national parks, off-road driving is illegal. Where off-road driving is allowed, the National Park Service regulates it.
